## 0. Purpose of This Document

Every security system has a threat model — a clear statement of what it protects against and what it does not. Systems that claim to protect against everything protect against nothing. This document states honestly what zk-poh prevents, what it cannot prevent, and what the tradeoffs are.

---

## 1. What We Are Protecting Against

### The Core Threat: Sybil Attacks

One entity controlling many identities to gain disproportionate influence:

- One person voting 1,000 times in a DAO
- One person claiming 500 airdrops
- One person draining a quadratic funding pool
- Bot armies manipulating votes or sentiment

The key property we need: **one human = one action per application**.

---

## 2. Threat Analysis

### 2.1 Bot Attacks

**Threat:** Automated scripts creating thousands of identities and submitting thousands of proofs.

**zk-poh defense:** Each identity must be registered on-chain by a trusted issuer. An attacker cannot self-register unlimited identities — they need issuer approval for each one. If `addHuman` is open (current demo), this defense is weakened. If `addHuman` is restricted to a trusted issuer, bots are blocked entirely.

**Status:** ✅ Prevented when issuer is restricted. ⚠️ Weakened in open demo mode.

---

### 2.2 Wallet Farming

**Threat:** One person creates 1,000 Ethereum wallets and tries to participate 1,000 times.

**zk-poh defense:** Having many wallets is irrelevant. The system does not care about wallet addresses. It cares about identity commitments registered by a trusted issuer. One person can only get one identity registered — the issuer controls this, not the blockchain.

**Status:** ✅ Prevented — wallet count is irrelevant.

---

### 2.3 Double Submission

**Threat:** One person tries to use the same proof twice in the same application.

**zk-poh defense:** The nullifier is recorded permanently after first use. Any attempt to reuse the same nullifier reverts. This is enforced at three layers: SimpleVoting, ProofOfHuman, and Semaphore. It is mathematically impossible to use the same nullifier twice.

**Status:** ✅ Prevented — mathematically guaranteed.

---

### 2.4 Cross-Application Tracking

**Threat:** An observer tries to link a person's activity across different applications. "The person who voted in App A is the same person who claimed the airdrop in App B."

**zk-poh defense:** The nullifier is derived from `identity + scope`. Different applications have different scopes, which produce completely different nullifiers. There is no mathematical relationship between nullifiers from different applications. An observer cannot link them.

**Status:** ✅ Prevented — mathematical property of the nullifier scheme.

---

### 2.5 Identity Revelation

**Threat:** Someone tries to figure out who voted how.

**zk-poh defense:** The ZK proof reveals nothing about the prover. The nullifier is a random-looking number with no connection to a name, wallet address, or any other identifier. The on-chain record shows only: "proof accepted, nullifier recorded." Nothing else.

**Status:** ✅ Prevented — zero-knowledge property of the proof.

---

### 2.6 Proof Forgery

**Threat:** An attacker tries to generate a valid proof without a registered identity.

**zk-poh defense:** The Groth16 proof system is computationally sound — generating a valid proof without knowing the private key is computationally infeasible given current cryptography. The verification keys were generated in a trusted setup ceremony. The math cannot be faked.

**Status:** ✅ Prevented — cryptographic guarantee.

---

### 2.7 Replay Attacks

**Threat:** An attacker captures a valid proof from one application and replays it in another.

**zk-poh defense:** The scope (application ID) is baked into the nullifier. A proof generated for App A with scope `500` will produce nullifier X. Submitting that proof to App B with scope `501` will produce nullifier Y — a different value. The proof will fail verification in App B because the scope in the proof doesn't match App B's expected scope.

**Status:** ✅ Prevented — scope binding.

## 3. What zk-poh Cannot Prevent

### 3.1 Credential Selling

**Threat:** A real human sells their private key to an attacker. The attacker uses the purchased key to generate valid proofs as if they were that human.

**Why zk-poh cannot prevent this:** The private key is valid. The proof it generates is valid. The system has no way to distinguish a proof generated by the original human from one generated by someone who bought the key.

**Mitigations (partial):**
- Each credential is limited to one use per application — the attacker needs one key per action, raising the cost
- The issuer can build in social verification that makes credential selling risky or traceable
- Biometric binding (not implemented) could tie keys to physical presence

**Status:** ⚠️ Not prevented. Cost of attack is raised but not eliminated.

---

### 3.2 Colluding Humans

**Threat:** Ten real humans coordinate their votes to unfairly influence an outcome.

**Why zk-poh cannot prevent this:** Each of the ten humans is a legitimate unique human. The system correctly verifies each one. The coordination is a social problem, not a cryptographic one.

**Mitigations (partial):**
- Quadratic voting reduces the power of coordinated groups
- Time locks and commit-reveal schemes reduce coordination effectiveness
- These are application-layer solutions, not zk-poh's responsibility

**Status:** ⚠️ Not prevented — out of scope by design.

---

### 3.3 Issuer Compromise

**Threat:** The trusted issuer is bribed, hacked, or coerced into registering fake identities.

**Why zk-poh cannot prevent this:** The issuer is trusted by definition. If the issuer is corrupt, they can register non-human or duplicate identities.

**Mitigations:**
- Use a decentralized issuer (a DAO vote on who gets registered)
- Use multiple independent issuers with threshold approval
- Integrate with established protocols (Gitcoin Passport, Proof of Humanity) that have their own Sybil resistance

**Status:** ⚠️ Depends on issuer integrity. Mitigable with decentralized issuance.

---

### 3.4 Lost or Stolen Private Keys

**Threat:** A user loses their private key. Now they cannot participate. Or their key is stolen and someone else participates in their name.

**Why this is hard:** Private key management is a fundamental problem in all of cryptography. There is no perfect solution.

**Mitigations (partial):**
- Social recovery schemes
- Wallet-derived identities (derive the ZK key from a hardware wallet signature)
- Key rotation (complex — requires the issuer to re-register)

**Status:** ⚠️ Known problem in all key-based systems. Mitigable but not eliminated.

---

### 3.5 Front-Running

**Threat:** An attacker sees a valid proof in the mempool and tries to use it before the original submitter.

**Why this is limited:** The nullifier in the proof is unique to the original identity. If the attacker submits the proof first, the original submitter's transaction will fail — but the attacker gains nothing because the proof is tied to the original identity's nullifier. The attacker cannot use the proof to act as a different identity.

**Real risk:** The original submitter's transaction gets front-run and fails, requiring resubmission. This is a denial-of-service risk, not an identity theft risk.

**Status:** ⚠️ DoS risk. Not an identity risk. Mitigable with private mempools or commit-reveal.

## 4. Trust Assumptions

For zk-poh to provide its guarantees, the following must hold:

| Assumption | What breaks if violated |
|---|---|
| Groth16 is computationally sound | Proofs can be forged |
| Trusted setup was conducted honestly | Proofs can be forged |
| Semaphore contracts are unmodified | Any guarantee |
| Issuer only registers real humans | Sybil resistance |
| User keeps private key secret | One-person-one-action |
| Ethereum is live and uncensored | Transactions can be submitted |

The first three are reasonable assumptions — Groth16 is well-studied, the Semaphore trusted setup was public, and the contracts are immutable on-chain.

The last three depend on behavior outside the cryptographic system.

## 5. Comparison to Alternatives

| Solution | Sybil Resistance | Privacy | Cost | Accessibility |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| KYC / Government ID | High | None | High | Excludes billions |
| Phone verification | Medium | Low | Low | Virtual numbers defeat it |
| Biometrics (Worldcoin) | High | Low | High hardware | People sell scans |
| Social graph (Gitcoin) | Medium | Medium | Low | Wealth-biased |
| **zk-poh** | **High (when issuer is honest)** | **High** | **Low** | **Anyone with a wallet** |

zk-poh is not the strongest possible Sybil resistance — a biometric system with liveness detection is harder to fake. But it offers the best combination of resistance, privacy, cost, and accessibility for most use cases.

---

## 6. Recommended Use Cases

**Well-suited:**
- DAO governance voting where members are known (issuer = DAO membership)
- Quadratic funding where donors self-attest
- Anonymous feedback systems
- Airdrop claiming where eligibility is pre-determined
- Rate limiting for services with known user bases

**Less suited:**
- High-stakes financial decisions where credential selling would be highly profitable
- Systems where the issuer is a single point of failure with no accountability
- Applications requiring liveness detection (proof the human is acting right now)

---

## 7. Summary

zk-poh provides strong cryptographic guarantees against automated Sybil attacks, double submission, cross-app tracking, identity revelation, and proof forgery. It cannot prevent credential selling, human collusion, or issuer compromise.

The honest answer: zk-poh raises the cost of Sybil attacks dramatically and eliminates the cheapest attacks entirely. For most applications, that is sufficient. For the highest-stakes applications, additional layers are needed.
